# Dead Drop

"They are lifting the veil in twelve minutes."

Renzo did not look up from the paper airplane folded into an origami raven. Its wings twitched in the ventilation draft. Fumiko slid a memo across the desk, her fingernails clicking like Morse code.

"You are joking."

"The Auditor General's office just requested Physicals for Sectors 4 through 7. Including the dead drops."

Renzo's voice stayed flat, but his thumb smudged the ink on the raven's beak. A smudge meant a misfire. A misfire meant a bullet.

Fumiko hissed. "We have had three years to digitize. Three years."

"Management prefers *tradition*." He said the word as if it had gone bad. "And the Union of Archivists still considers PDFs unproven technology."

The clock above the water cooler flicked to 10:47.

They moved in tandem, a ballet of paranoia. Renzo fed documents into the shredder, which spat confetti into the air like a defeated celebration. Fumiko crawled under the Chief Administrator's desk and pried loose a false panel behind the left leg. Inside, a yellowed envelope pulsed faintly, its edges crisping as if scorched by invisible eyes.

"It is active," she muttered. "Why is it active?"

"Maybe the thing wants to be found." Renzo tossed her a pair of rubber gloves. "Maybe it knows what quarterlies do to morale."

She glared. "This is not funny."

"No," he agreed, "but the part where we get prosecuted for obstructing bureaucratic transparency will be hilarious."

At 10:53, they found the last drop: a child's diary hidden inside a ream of copy paper. The entries detailed crop rotations on a commune in Nebraska. The final page held a crayon drawing of a sun wearing sunglasses.

"Is this..."

"A dead drop? Yes. Dangerous? Probably. Sensible? Never was." Renzo tucked it into his jacket. "Come on. The Auditor is going to love the stapler."

The stapler was a relic, predating even the Union's strictures. Its base unscrewed to reveal a hollow chamber. Inside, microfilm spools turned lazily, each frame a recipe for a different kind of silence.

At 11:00, the lights buzzed. A woman in a gray suit marched in, flanked by two interns clutching clipboards. The Auditor General had a face like a closed filing cabinet.

"Mr. Renzo. Ms. Fumiko. I trust you are ready for inspection."

Renzo smiled. It looked like a knife wrapped in cellophane.

"We have been ready since 1997."

They spent the next hour watching her sift through trivialities: supply requests, birthday cards, a memo about the coffee machine's existential crisis. The Auditor hummed as she worked, a sound like distant fax machines.

When she left, Fumiko exhaled. "She did not even check the ceiling tiles."

"Of course not. She was here for the show." Renzo pulled the raven from his pocket and smoothed its wings. "The real audit starts when she realizes the building's blueprints do not include this room."

He lobbed the paper airplane out the window. It soared, then dove into a storm drain.

Fumiko watched it vanish. "What was in that one?"

"The recipe for decent coffee."

She snorted. "We should have used the stapler."

The clock hit noon. Somewhere inside the walls, a pipe began to laugh.
