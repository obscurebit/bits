# Frostbite Protocol

The ice crackled like radio static as Dr. Ewa Nowak scraped her scalpel along the subject's femur. The exposed bone was blackened and crystalline, translucent in places, as if light itself had given up trying to pass through.

Her gloved hand trembled. Not from cold. From the implications.

"Temperature?"

"Minus thirty-two and dropping," Tariq Hansen said, his voice muffled by the heating element in his parka. The corporal watched the sensor array around the excavation pit, its wires disappearing into the permafrost like roots. "They are waking up."

*They*. Not remains. Not specimens. The word had slipped into their vocabulary during the first thaw cycle, when the bodies in the ice began to answer touch.

Ewa did not correct him. Her throat felt packed with snow.

The subject's eye flickered. A film of ice split over its pupil, revealing a milky blue iris. Ewa dropped the scalpel. It rang against the frozen ground and the wind took the sound away.

Notes from the 1962 file moved through her mind in fluorescent fragments: *Project Permafrost will test cryonic preservation for tactical deployment. Subjects should be revivable within twenty-four hours of thaw.* Lies, of course. The Cold War had been a graveyard of such ambitions. But the Soviets had buried something here: men wired to machines that hummed under the Arctic for more than sixty years.

The subject sat up.

Frost sloughed from its shoulders. Skin peeled away in translucent sheets, revealing muscle that flexed in the dark. Tariq stepped back, rifle raised.

"Don't shoot," Ewa said.

The subject turned. Its neck bent too far, ligaments creaking under the ice. It fixed Ewa with its blue eye and shaped one word through broken lips.

"Zhdite."

Tariq's finger tightened on the trigger. "What is it saying?"

"Wait," Ewa said. "It means wait."

The subject's mouth opened again. Static came out first, then a child's laugh, then a voice in careful, accented English.

"Freezing preserves. It does not absolve."

Ewa went cold in a way the parka could not answer. The voice was not her father's, exactly. It had his pauses. His careful contempt for fear.

He had died in 1989, or so the family file said: dissident poet, Dresden, collision with a Stasi van, closed coffin.

The subject lurched forward. Tariq fired. The bullet struck its shoulder, and the body shuddered, shedding ice like armor. Beneath the frost was not a monster. It was a man.

"Stop." Ewa stepped between them. "He was a soldier."

The subject caught her wrist.

The cold went through her marrow. Images arrived with the touch: a lab lit by failing fluorescents; conscripts strapped to steel tables; a young man with her father's mouth biting through a leather restraint while a scientist pressed a syringe against his neck.

*You will sleep,* the scientist told him, *and when you wake, the war will be over.*

Ewa gasped. The subject released her and folded into the snow. His body began to flake apart, ash-white and weightless.

Tariq stared into the pit, where a dozen more figures shifted beneath the ice as if remembering orders.

"What do we do?"

Ewa picked up the scalpel. It was useless now.

"We listen."

The first subject's eye froze shut. His final word came almost gently.

"Forgive."

The wind carried it away, a prayer to no one.
