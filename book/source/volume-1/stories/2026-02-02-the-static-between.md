# The Static Between

Amara adjusted the receiver's dial, leaving a crescent of dust on her thumb. The attic clicked and breathed around her: old transformers, stacked hobbyist gear, her grandfather's handwritten labels curling off plastic bins. She had come to salvage what she could before the estate sale, but the radio's hiss stopped her with one hand on the plug.

Beneath the static was a rhythm: three clicks, a pause, five clicks, another pause. Prime numbers. Not random.

She leaned closer, the headphones tight against her ears. The pattern shifted into tones that rose and fell like a question. Her dry-erase board already held two pages of notes. The signal was local, or pretending to be. The towers she checked did not match its frequency.

When Diego called, she let it ring. His text followed: *Still chasing ghosts?*

The next night, the signal changed.

It began with white noise, then a voice: distorted, genderless, speaking in a language that was not quite language. Amara worked the dials until fragments surfaced inside it: a child laughing, a train whistle, a thin run of jazz. Then silence.

Then her own voice, from three days ago, muttering *"Dammit, why won't you-"*

She froze. The receiver was not only picking up her words. It was returning them.

---

Diego met her at the old industrial park, skeptical until she played the recording. "Okay," he said, adjusting his glasses. "That's weird."

They followed the signal to a rusted water tower behind a shuttered factory. Amara's Geiger counter ticked steadily: normal background radiation. Diego climbed first, the rungs slick with dew. At the top they found no antenna, no dish, no transmitter. Just a corroded platform and a single fiber-optic cable snaking into the ground.

"It's not transmitting," Diego said. "It's *receiving*."

Amara knelt and brushed moss from the connector. "Or bridging."

The signal started again as they descended. This time it was a phrase, repeated in perfect English:

*"Do you hear us?"*

---

They answered.

Diego rigged a microphone to the transmitter. Amara hesitated, then said, "Yes."

The reply came at once: *"You are late."*

Late for what? They asked, but the voice only cycled through fragments: a ticking clock, a woman reciting pi, a dog barking behind a closed door. When Amara demanded clarity, the signal collapsed into static.

Days passed. The tower went silent.

Then, at 3 a.m., Amara's phone buzzed with a coordinates link. Diego's name was on the message. He had not sent it.

The location was a field outside town, overgrown with weeds. In the center stood a structure they had never noticed before: a low, curved arch, like a ribcage half-buried in earth. Its surface looked metallic, but when Amara touched it, it warmed and gave slightly, like skin.

Diego tapped his flashlight against it. The sound echoed hollow. "It's not on any survey," he whispered.

They returned each night. The arch did not change. The signal did not return.

But sometimes, when the wind shifted, Amara felt a vibration in her molars, just beyond hearing.

She stopped sleeping.

---

Diego found her at the arch one morning, asleep against its base.

"You look terrible," he said.

"You didn't feel it?" she asked. "Last night. Like a pause in everything."

He shook his head.

The next day, the arch was gone. Only a circle of dead grass remained.

Amara still checks the receiver every evening. The static is just static now.

But sometimes, when she is alone, she hums a tune she has never heard before, and the air seems to listen.
