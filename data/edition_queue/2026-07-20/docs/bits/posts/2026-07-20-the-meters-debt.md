# The Meter’s Debt

Rule 14.7: *All water consumption figures must be entered into the Master Meter before 1700 hours. Failure to comply will result in ration deduction.*  

We always hit the 1700 deadline, no matter what. Even on days the sun buckled the asphalt outside or the frogs fell during rain season. The Master Meter sat in its plexiglass cubby like a saint in a shrine, its cracked digital face flickering numbers only Greta could decipher. She’d been there when they installed it, back when the city still sent inspectors and the pipes didn’t hiss like sick animals.  

*“It’s just a counter,”* the new ones said. The ones who didn’t remember the Summer of Stagnation, when the meter’s first iteration started logging phantom usage—thousands of liters supposedly drawn by empty buildings. Greta had watched her brother, Finn, rewire it himself, his fingers stained with copper and sweat. *“Tell them what they want to see,”* he’d said. *“Keep the real numbers in your head.”*  

That was before Finn dissolved into the background noise of the Collapse. Before Greta became the only one who knew why the workaround existed. Before the meter started storing more than numbers.  

We called it a glitch when it began replaying old entries. A 2019 usage spike from the laundromat that no longer existed. A 2023 surge from the school that burned down. Greta would shoulder her way to the cubby, her bad knee throbbing, and jab the reset button until the screen flashed *CORRECTED*. *“It’s just tired,”* she’d mutter. *“Old things get tired.”*  

Then Harlan started his habit. Every Friday, he’d punch in an extra zero, bloating his zone’s numbers by ten percent. A joke, he said. A way to “balance the scales” since his sector had the most leaks. We laughed. We covered for him. Even Greta didn’t mind, not really. Letting Harlan’s fudges slide felt like keeping a candle lit in a dark room.  

But the meter remembered.  

It surfaced during the Audit of the Unseen, when the Regional Compliance Officer arrived with her thumb-drive and a nose for discrepancies. She wanted the Master Meter’s physical logs—paper printouts from the last five years. Greta stalled, citing the flu, the power flickers, her dead brother’s birthday. *“Just one more day,”* she begged her nephew, Jace, who’d started tagging along to “learn the ropes.” Jace, whose ration card was up for renewal.  

*“Aunt Greta,”* he said, his voice soft as a dust motes, *“they’re going to find out anyway.”*  

We gathered when the Officer finally pulled the logs. The paper curled at the edges, ink smudged from a hundred sweaty hands. But there it was: Harlan’s Friday zeros, blooming like tumors in the data. The Officer’s eyes narrowed. *“Explain this pattern.”*  

Harlan stepped forward, all charm and calloused hands. *“Must be a mistake—”*  

*“The meter doesn’t make mistakes,”* Greta snapped. Too loud. Too sharp.  

The Officer turned to her, intrigued. *“You’re the one who maintains it.”*  

*“I maintain the *process*,”* Greta said. *“The meter maintains itself.”*  

A lie. They all knew it. The machine was a corpse kept upright by duct tape and superstition.  

Jace spoke for the first time, his voice cracking. *“She’s been covering his falsifications because Harlan’s sister works in Rations. If his zone looks like it’s wasting water, her kids lose their supplement.”*  

The room stilled. Harlan’s face went the color of old rice.  

Greta’s knees went weak. Jace wasn’t supposed to know. Finn’s promise had been hers alone: *Keep the zeros alive. Keep the supplements flowing.*  

The Officer closed her thumb-drive. *“I’ll need to see the physical meter.”*  

We followed her to the cubby. The screen was dark. Greta thumped it once, twice. Nothing.  

Then the backlight flickered. Numbers scrolled—fast, frantic. The room’s entries, the real ones, the ones Finn had taught her to hide:  

**2019: Laundromat 42,000L**  
**2023: School 118,000L**  
**2027: Harlan’s Zone 230,000L (adjusted to 23,000L)**  
**2027: Greta’s Zone 15,000L (adjusted to 150,000L)**  

The last line pulsed.  

*“What is this?”* the Officer breathed.  

*“The truth,”* Greta said.  

Harlan’s eyes darted to his sister’s children, waiting at home. Jace stared at his feet. The Officer reached for her comms device.  

Greta’s hand landed on the reset button. She pressed it.  

The screen went blank.  

*“Try again,”* the Officer ordered.  

Greta pressed it nine more times. Nothing.  

*“It’s dead,”* Harlan whispered.  

*“Convenient,”* the Officer said.  

But we knew. The meter hadn’t been alive for years. It was just a vault. A tomb. A promise.  

Jace broke the silence. *“Aunt Greta? You didn’t have to do it for me.”*  

The words hung. The Officer’s comms stayed silent. Harlan’s shoulders slumped.  

Greta looked at the dead screen and thought of Finn, his hands on the wires, his last laugh like a spark in the dark.  

*“No,”* she said, and the warmth that had curdled all day finally cooled into something like truth. *“But I did.”*
