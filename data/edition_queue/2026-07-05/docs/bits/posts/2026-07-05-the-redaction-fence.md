# The Redaction Fence

Rosa’s clipboard hated lies. Not the big ones—people claimed all sorts of nonsense to jump housing lists—but the small, daily omissions. Like how the Sánchez kid’s asthma flared when the subway rumble shook their temporary billet, or how Mr. Okoro’s “retirement hobby” was actually fencing smuggled medicals through the old sewer access under his tomato garden. The clipboard surfaced these truths as paperwork. Not holograms or blinking alerts, but actual paper, yellowed at the edges, as if pulled from a file cabinet in a future where every omission had already been punished.  

She’d found it in a busted vending machine behind the permit office, still warm, next to a bag of lime-chili chips that hadn’t been made since the factory fire. The first time it spat out a form—*Notice of Structural Unfitness, effective 6 months hence*—she’d laughed. Then the building collapsed. Now she carried it everywhere, the cracked leather case slapping against her hip like a holster.  

The problem today was the Chuang family. Their application was flawless: three kids, a grandmother on dialysis, a waiting list stamp from the mayor’s office itself. But the clipboard hummed when she reviewed their site assignment. A form materialized, *Certificate of Demolition, pending toxic soil findings*, dated next week. Rosa knew the lot—it abutted the old tannery, where her cousin had worked until the acid leaks started showing up in his urine. The city wanted the land for a high-rise, but the Chungs needed a home.  

“You’re stalling,” said Councilman Varga, leaning over her desk, cologne sharp enough to strip paint. “The mayor’s watching this one. Family’s a ‘model of resilience.’” He smiled like he’d coined the phrase.  

Rosa didn’t smile back. She’d seen his name on a future form too: *Warrant for Misappropriation of Reclamation Funds*. But that was six months out. Plenty of time for him to retire rich.  

At the site, she found Mrs. Chuang kneeling in the dirt, planting marigolds around exposed pipes. “They said it’s safe,” she called, voice brittle. “The children need roots.”  

Rosa crouched, showing her the demolition notice. The woman’s face went the color of wet cement. “But… we signed everything. Paid the fee.”  

“Your fee,” Rosa said, “is why they won’t stop it. They’ll let you build, then blame ‘unforeseen toxins.’”  

The woman’s hands twisted her apron. “What do you want?”  

“Not me. The clipboard.” Rosa pressed the device to the ground. It shuddered, spitting out a new sheet: *Liability Waiver, signed by all occupants prior to occupancy*. A get-out-of-jail-free card for the city.  

Mrs. Chuang recoiled. “You want us to sign away our right to complain?”  

“I want you to have a house,” Rosa said. “Choose when the lie collapses.”  

That night, Councilman Varga cornered her at the bus stop. “You approved the Chung permit. Without the waiver.”  

“The paperwork came through clean.”  

“Your clipboard’s a relic. A glitch.”  

“Then why’d you just pay off your mistress with city funds?” Rosa held up the device. A new form glowed: *Affidavit of Adultery, submitted by anonymous source*.  

Varga paled. “You wouldn’t.”  

“I’m just a clerk.” She slipped the form into his pocket. “But the future’s full of eyes.”  

The Chungs moved in. Rosa marked their file *Completed—No Issues*. The clipboard stayed silent for weeks.  

Then, one morning, it spat out a photo: Mrs. Chuang’s marigolds, now withered, and a child’s sneaker floating in a puddle of iridescent sludge.  

Rosa added a footnote to the day’s reports: *Site stability compromised. Recommend relocation. No available funds.*  

She kept the photo. Some truths were better left as paper.
