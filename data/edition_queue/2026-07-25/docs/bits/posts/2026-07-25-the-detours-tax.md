# The Detour's Tax

The shortest path always costs the most. Rosa knew this as she adjusted her delivery satchel, its straps frayed from years of shouldering packages through the city’s shifting grid. Elm Street’s closure still gnawed at her—a decade-old wreck she’d witnessed, a truck jackknifed across the bike lane, its cargo of liquid nitrogen hissing into the gutters. New drivers took the detour as gospel; Rosa alone remembered the bloodstained pavement, the reason the union hall’s route chart avoided Elm like a curse.  

Inside the hall, the posted schedule glowed faintly, its ink shifting like a snake shedding skin. The board answered questions, but only if you paid. Rosa’s coworker Marcus jabbed a finger at the chart, his knuckles bruised from years of jamming mislabeled parcels into lockers. “It’s rigged,” he said. “They’re making us run marathons to deliver toothpaste.” Lila, her hair tucked under a union beanie, rolled her eyes. “You want to fight the algorithm? Start by showing up sober.”  

Rosa stayed silent. The board had taken her voice once, back when she’d asked for the fastest route during her daughter’s asthma attack. The answer came—*Take the 7th Ave bridge*—but left her mute for three days, her throat raw as a scraped knee. Now she wrote questions on sticky notes, peeling them off in hieroglyphs of desperation.  

Outside, the committee chair, Ms. Voss, watched from a electric cart, her clipboard blinking. She’d been waiting for Rosa to slip, to prove the old routes were obsolete. The debt collectors had started circling too, ever since Rosa’s ex-husband skipped town with their savings. She needed the overtime Marcus despised, the extra laps through the industrial park where the rats outnumbered the streetlights.  

When the board rerouted her to Elm at 3 p.m., Rosa balked. The ink recoiled, forming a question mark that dripped like wax. *Why?* she scrawled, tearing off the note. The board pulsed. A memory surfaced: her father teaching her to ride a bike on Elm, his hands steady on the seat. Gone. The route flashed green.  

Marcus caught her at the loading dock. “You’re taking Elm?” “It’s faster,” she said, her throat itching. He grimaced. “That street’s a tomb.” Lila appeared, waving a manifest. “The board’s been lying about fuel costs. I cross-checked the invoices.” Marcus snorted. “Invoices? This isn’t a bank, Lila. It’s a *favor shop*.”  

Rosa’s phone buzzed—a debt reminder. She peeled another note: *How to pay what I owe?* The board shuddered. A memory of her mother’s hands, kneading dough for olives, vanished. The answer appeared: *Three detours for one debt erased.*  

She chose Elm twice more, each pass erasing a chunk of her past: her daughter’s first steps, the smell of her father’s cologne, the exact shade of her mother’s wedding dress. Ms. Voss nodded approval as Rosa’s satchel lightened, the debt counter ticking downward.  

At shift’s end, Marcus and Lila cornered her. “We’re filing a grievance,” Lila said. “The board’s a parasite.” “It’s just the rules,” Rosa muttered, her head buzzing. Marcus spat. “Your rules. My kid’s got pneumonia because I can’t afford meds.”  

Rosa reached for her pen to sign the grievance form—only to find it gone. The board had taken it, of course. Her favorite, a stubby purple thing with a cracked rollerball, now nestled in its guts. She borrowed Lila’s cheap Bic, the tines scratching.  

As they walked out, Lila joked, “You look like you’ve lost your last marble.”  

Rosa smiled. The pen had been her father’s.  

It hurt, but only a little. Like a stone in your shoe you learn to walk around.
