# The Logbook of Minor Grievances

"You’re still here?" says the man in the chipped yellow raincoat, his voice a file stripping paint. He stands at the intake desk, one finger pressed to the logbook’s open page, as if it might bite.  

You don’t answer. You’re flipping through the day’s drop-offs: a VHS tape labeled *EVIDENCE*, a shoebox of receipts from a defunct pawnshop, a child’s backpack stuffed with mismatched mittens. The logbook lies between you and the raincoat man, its pages ruled with the ghost of a typewriter’s tread. It records everything. Except what matters.  

"Last Tuesday," he says, "I filed a grievance about the rats. They’re eating the old movie posters in Storage C. You haven’t logged it."  

You have. You remember the way his handwriting loops like a slinky, how he spelled *rodents* with two ‘d’s. But the logbook only acknowledges complaints from people who don’t live here. Who don’t need to. The system works perfectly for the wrong person.  

Your supervisor, Lenny, taught you this trick: when someone challenges the logbook, show them the proof. You flip to the entry yourself. Blank lines stare back. The raincoat man’s face reddens. "See?" he says. "It’s broken. Again."  

You want to tell him it’s not the logbook. It’s the whole building, the whole neighborhood, rigged to listen only to those who can afford to be ignored. Instead, you hand him a numbered queue slip. "Come back tomorrow."  

He storms off, raincoat flapping like a deflated sail. You watch security footage on the grainy CRT monitor—1997, the year the internet came on like a fever dream, and this place became a museum of things people swore they’d return for.  

Three years ago, Lenny pressed the logbook into your hands during your first week. "It’s a sieve," he said. "But the wrong kind. Keeps the big things, leaks the small. You’ll get good at knowing which is which." He retired last spring, leaving you to the daily calculus of whose grief gets a line entry and whose doesn’t.  

The phone rings. You answer.  

"Mara?" A woman’s voice, frayed at the edges. Mrs. Chaudhry from 4B. "The logbook won’t take my complaint about the smell in the stairwell. It says I’m not a priority."  

You don’t tell her the logbook decided this six months ago, the day her son stopped paying his storage fee. You don’t tell her it’s why you stay—to manually override the entries, to scribble *Urgent* in the margins when no one’s looking.  

"Bring me a sample," you say. "From the stairwell. I’ll log it myself."  

She thanks you, and the gratitude sits like a stone in your throat. You hate it. You live for it.  

That night, you dream of the logbook as a ledger of all you’ve erased: the mold in 7A, the broken lock on the bike shed, the way the tenants in the front apartments never complain because they know how easily a "non-priority" can become a locked door, a padlock, a yellow FOR RENT sign.  

You wake to the alarm. Not the fire alarm—a deeper, guttural buzz. The intake room.  

The raincoat man stands over the logbook, a crowbar in his hand. Pages torn, scattered. "It’s rigged," he hisses. "You’re rigging it."  

You don’t call security. You don’t point out that he’s not even a tenant anymore, that his grievance should be logged under *Former Residents (Do Not Prioritize)*. Instead, you hand him a pen.  

"Write it yourself," you say. "Date it. Sign it."  

He does. The logbook accepts the page without complaint.  

At dawn, you find Mrs. Chaudhry’s sample on the desk: a mason jar half-filled with sludge the color of a bruise. You log it under *Biohazard – Immediate Action Required*. The logbook doesn’t argue.  

You tender your resignation the next day. The new manager, a woman with a clipboard and a beeper, doesn’t ask why. She just hands you a final queue slip.  

"Your exit interview," she says. "Number 47."  

You pocket it. Walk out.  

The raincoat man is in the lobby, arguing with a moving truck driver. You slip the queue slip into his hand.  

"For the rats," you say.  

He looks at you, startled. Then nods.  

You don’t look back. The logbook stays behind, hungry and precise, recording everything except the weight of who gets to speak.
