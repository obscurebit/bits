# The Apology Coin

Greta adjusted her municipal lanyard and stared at the damp spot on her desk where her brother’s elbow had rested. “It’s not even a real favor,” he’d said, scratching the mole behind his ear—the same nervous habit he’d had since we stole Mrs. Kowalski’s tulips in third grade. “Just bump my number up. No one’ll notice.” He slid the crumpled queue ticket across the laminated map of Service District 4. “I’ll owe you one.”  

The ticket smelled like stale fries.  

At her workstation, the Apology Coin glinted in its plastic tray. Not a coin, really—more like a thick button, edge-to-edge with concentric rings, warm to the touch. City policy: one per district, for resolving disputes that “threatened civic harmony.” Greta had used it twice in seven years. Once to pacify a man who’d sworn the pothole outside his laundromat was a government surveillance pit. Once to calm a crowd that’d gathered because the number 7 bus had skipped its route for three days straight. Both times, the Coin had pulsed brighter when she pressed it to a ticket, and the complainant left murmuring, eyes glazed, as if they’d suddenly remembered their mother’s voice.  

The queue behind her brother groaned. A woman in a velour tracksuit glared at his ticket now lying on Greta’s desk. “He just got here,” she said, loud enough to sting.  

Greta’s finger hovered. The Coin’s practical question: *Could* she bump him? Answer: yes. Ethical question: What about the woman whose turn it actually was? The one whose hands trembled from either rage or withdrawal, depending on which rumor you believed.  

Her brother texted: *cmon gret. its just this once.*  

She thought of the last time he’d repaid a debt—returning her borrowed car with a dent and a gas tank on empty, saying, “You’re family. What’s the worst I could do?”  

The Coin thrummed against her palm.  

“Next!” she called, waving forward the tracksuit woman. “Issue with your sanitation pickup?”  

Her brother’s ticket stayed on the desk.  

When the Coin went dark at 3:17 PM, Greta knew. The city had deactivated it remotely. Someone in the back office had noticed the irregularity, or maybe the queue’s collective glare had achieved sentience. Her brother’s number flickered off the digital board.  

He stormed in at 4:30, hair sticking up like he’d run all the way from the bus stop. “You didn’t—”  

“I followed protocol,” she said, handing him a printed complaint form. Her voice stayed level, that practiced municipal calm. But her knuckles whitened.  

He snatched the paper. “This is bullshit.”  

A new notice glowed on her screen: *Effective immediately, Apology Coin usage requires dual authorization. All prior permissions revoked pending review.*  

Greta leaned back. Let him read it. Let him notice the date stamp—yesterday’s date—and understand that someone, somewhere, had already decided to neuter the Coin. That the city had turned her tool into a paperweight just in time to watch him squirm.  

“You’re welcome,” she said, because it felt like a lie he deserved.  

Outside, the queue shifted like a living thing, its morality intact.
