### The Ozone Clerk and the Borrowed Generator

The air in the equipment rental shed tasted like burnt cotton and regret. That was the ozone, mostly—old generators sparking their last—but also Mrs. Peet’s lavender air freshener, which she’d hung from the ceiling fan despite the NO OPEN FLAMES sign. I’d worked the counter three years and still couldn’t smell the difference between the machines dying and the woman trying to perfume them alive.  

“Your mother’s generator again,” Mrs. Peet said, sliding the maintenance tag across the counter. Her voice was a wet cough. “Third time this month.”  

The tag was bent at the corner, the kind of yellow that used to mean “caution” before everyone started using it for “broken.” I flipped it over. The ink had shifted. Last week it read *Needs New Spark Plug (Cost: $45.00)*. Now it said *Needs New Spark Plug (Cost: $45.00, Plus One Favor Owed to Clerk)*.  

I kept my face flat. Mrs. Peet’s son had drowned in the harbor two years back, and since then she’d treated every interaction like a negotiation with fate. She brought cookies when she returned tools early. She “forgot” to sign the liability waivers. I’d started leaving her generators out front so she wouldn’t have to ask.  

“You know the rules,” I said. “Maintenance fees apply.”  

She smiled, a crack in her lipstick. “And you know my boy used to fix these things for free.”  

The tag quivered. I didn’t look at it. Mrs. Peet’s grandson was in the hospital again, lungs full of the same damp that rotted the shed’s walls. I’d seen the GoFundMe on her phone when she handed it to me to “check the weather.”  

“I can waive the fee,” I said, “if you sign the equipment over to me after.”  

Her eyes narrowed. The tag now read *Needs New Spark Plug (Clerk’s Terms Apply)*.  

“Done,” she said.  

At home, Mom was measuring pills into a Tic Tac tin. “You’re late,” she said. “I told you I’d pick up the dry cleaning.”  

“You don’t drive,” I said.  

“I could’ve walked.” She spat a pill into her palm. “But you’re always here now, so why bother?”  

The generator from Mrs. Peet sat in the corner, humming like a contented cat. I’d traded a favor owed for it, though I hadn’t told Mrs. Peet what I wanted yet. The tag had changed again in my car: *Needs New Spark Plug (Favor Redeemed: One Dry Cleaning Run)*.  

Mom stared at it. “Is that… hers?”  

“Mine now,” I said.  

She laughed, sharp as a wire. “You think that makes you better than her? Trading grief like it’s currency?”  

The tag flared: *Needs New Spark Plug (Truth in Exchange for Silence)*.  

I yanked the plug from the generator. It was pristine. No carbon, no wear. Just a piece of metal shaped like a promise.  

Mom’s hands trembled. “You wouldn’t—”  

I held it up. “What? Use the thing the way it’s meant to be used? You’re right. I’m not better. But I’m tired of pretending I don’t know how this works.”  

She looked away. The dry cleaning hung on the door like a ghost.  

“I’ll do the laundry,” she whispered.  

The tag went blank.  

I didn’t tell her about the promotion I’d get when Mrs. Peet’s generator powered the whole block during the next blackout. Some advantages, you keep to yourself.
