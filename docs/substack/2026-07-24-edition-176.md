### The Token Wash

The token is warm when I palm it, still holding the heat from my brother’s fist. It’s a dull brass disc, edge worn smooth, number 47 stamped unevenly. The woman in the peeling vinyl chair taps her heels faster, watching me like she knows I’m stalling. Her son’s fever burned through three tokens already this week.  

“I can give you one more,” I say, thumbing the edge of 47. “But it’ll come out of next month’s allotment.”  

She nods, chin quivering. Behind her, my brother coughs—a wet, theatrical sound. He’s been waiting to swap his day’s tokens for a rail voucher, but I haven’t stamped his form yet.  

*He’s useful*, I think, *but not kind*.  

---  

I remember when tokens were just tokens. Before the clinics started weighing them against body mass, before the transit office began cross-referencing numbers with hospital admittance logs. Back when Dad ran the exchange booth, he’d let us trade extras for candy at the newsstand. Now, every token is a ledger entry, a unit of blood and breath.  

Mama’s workaround was genius, once. If you washed a token in the staff bathroom sink—three scrubs under hot water, no more—it’d lose its magnetic charge just long enough to slip through the system unnoticed. We called it “baptizing” them. Bought us weeks of breathing room when the allotments shrank.  

But then Jacek from Maintenance tried to baptize twelve tokens at once. Overloaded the whole grid. The feedback fried his left hand, and the clinic made us pay for the replacement prosthetic in tokens.  

---  

“You’re doing it again,” my brother hisses, cornering me at the filing cabinet. “That woman’s son is a drain. You give her 47, and I don’t get my voucher.”  

“You’re welcome to explain that to their corpse,” I snap, but he’s right. The system’s tighter now. No more blind spots. If I rewrite the allocation, it’ll flag. And if it flags, they’ll audit. And if they audit…  

I think of Mama’s notebook, hidden under the floorboard at home. The one where she wrote, *“The machine doesn’t care who it breaks, as long as it breaks evenly.”*  

---  

The officer from the Compliance Bureau watches me from the doorway, leather notebook open. She’s new. Doesn’t know about the baptism trick. Doesn’t know I’ve already swapped 47 with a scrubbed token from my pocket.  

“Procedure,” she says, “requires all exchanges to be logged before dispersal.”  

“Procedure,” I say, “requires you to have a warrant before you step behind my counter.”  

She flushes, but steps back. The woman with the sick son takes her token and leaves. My brother storms out, slamming the door.  

---  

I find him later at the bus dock, trading his unstamped voucher to a man with a suitcase full of unofficial tokens.  

“You’ll get us both shut down,” I say.  

“He paid extra,” my brother says. “And you’re still washing tokens like it’s 1968.”  

I don’t tell him the baptism worked today. That 47 is safely in the clinic’s system, buying the boy antibiotics. I don’t tell him I found Mama’s notebook missing a page last week.  

Instead, I toss my scrubbed token into the ash tray. It clinks against others like it—dead, hollow, clean.  

---  

The officer returns at dusk. No warrant, but a raffle ticket in her hand.  

“Draw 227,” she says. “Winner gets a bypass code. Bypasses the clinic’s token lock.”  

I stare. “You’re offering me a cheat.”  

“Call it a test.” She smiles. “See if you’ll break the system to save someone.”  

I take the ticket. The number bleeds blue ink.  

At home, I match it against Mama’s notebook. The missing page had a similar stain.  

I burn both in the kitchen sink, watching the ink swirl into the drain.  

---  

The next morning, the officer’s body is found in the transit tunnel. Official cause: exposure. But her notebook’s missing, and her raffle ticket’s in my brother’s pocket.  

He grins. “Turns out tokens aren’t the only thing you can wash.”  

I don’t laugh.  

The woman’s son lives.  

I baptize three tokens that week.  

The system holds.  

For now.
