## 📖 Today's Bit

### The Glint in the Chart

The fluorescent lights buzzed like a trapped wasp, and the air smelled of lemon disinfectant and burnt coffee. I sorted memory logs at my desk, each one a translucent ribbon tagged with a name and a date. My fingers knew the weights: a first communion, a divorce decree, a child’s first steps—all compressed into strips that curled at the edges if left out too long.  

Marco leaned against the snack cart, his name tag—*M. Delp*—faded to a ghost of its former self. “You still using that brick from 2007?” he asked, nodding at the log reader clutched in my hand. Its screen flickered, stubborn as a mule.  

“It works,” I said. “ Doesn’t try to upsell me grief counseling.”  

The Access Chart hung on the wall behind him, its grid of permissions glowing amber. It updated in real-time, rows shifting like a snake shedding skin. Need-based now. If your pulse hit the right threshold—*genuine distress, verifiable trauma*—the chart would blink green and spit out a token. No human gatekeeper. No me.  

Marco’s eyes tracked the chart. “They’re decommissioning the old readers next week. You’ve got two choices: retrain for terminal support or take the exit package.”  

I didn’t respond. My reader was already warm against my palm, humming a low C that only I could hear.  

He left. I waited until his shoes scuffed out of range before pulling up the log tagged *Delp, M.*—a memory from seven years back. In it, Marco’s sister testified against our supervisor during the union audit. She’d been crying, her voice a frayed thread. I’d been the one to verify her memory’s authenticity, to stamp it *UNALTERED*. They fired her anyway. Marco never spoke of it. Neither did I.  

The chart flickered. A new row appeared: *Delp, M.—Access Granted (Trauma: Moderate)*.  

He was back within the hour, holding a coffee and a folded receipt. “I need to see her testimony,” he said. “The one you certified.”  

“You can just ask the chart now,” I said.  

“I did. It says *Access Pending*. Keeps giving me a error about ‘emotional proximity.’” He tossed the receipt onto my desk. “You take this. For the… trouble.”  

It was a voucher for a memory revision. His treat.  

I unspooled Marco’s log, the ribbon catching the light like a thread of spider silk. His sister’s face filled the reader’s screen, younger and fiercer. “They’re revising her testimony,” Marco muttered. “Saying she recanted. Saying she lied.”  

The chart pulsed amber. *Access Denied (Insufficient Need).*  

Marco’s jaw twitched. “You could override it. You remember how.”  

I did. The old protocol—*bypass the chart’s algorithm, input a manual verification code*—still lived in my muscle memory. But the system would flag it. They’d know.  

“You want me to risk my exit package so you can feel like a hero?” I said.  

“No,” he said. “I want you to do it because you know the difference between a revision and a lie.”  

The reader buzzed, the log warming to a memory I’d archived years ago: Marco slipping me a inhaler during a blackouts, his sister pressing a bruised shoulder against mine at a union rally. Loyalty, like guilt, is a debt that accrues.  

I entered the code. The chart screamed—*UNAUTHORIZED ACTION DETECTED*—and the lights dimmed to emergency red. Marco’s token spat out, his sister’s testimony intact.  

He didn’t thank me. Just pocketed the token and left.  

HR arrived at dawn. I handed them my reader, its screen cracked from where I’d thrown it against the wall.  

“Exit package,” I said.  

The chart glowed serenely, its rows steady and untroubled.  

I keep the receipt Marco gave me. It’s tissue-thin, but it smells like him: stale hazelnut and cheap cologne. Sometimes I wonder if the revision voucher is still good. If he’d take it back, knowing I’m not worth the debt.  

I don’t use it.  

Some refusals are the only thing left that feels like a self.
