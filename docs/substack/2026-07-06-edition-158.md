### Maintenance Tag 217B

We kept the building running on a schedule we all knew was a lie.  

The tags were supposed to stay put. That was the rule. Painted numbers on the service doors, handwritten notes tucked under door mats, the yellowing stickers on the ancient HVAC units in the basement—each one a promise that the system worked. But Tag 217B had other plans.  

It started small. Luisa from 4B called to say her showerhead was speaking in Morse code. We sent Mario, who knew the pipes better than anyone, and he found the tag stuck to the shower arm—217B, its numbers smudged but legible. By the time he peeled it off, the tag had tripled in size. Mario dropped it in his toolbox, figured it was a fluke.  

Then the calls came in waves.  

Tag 217B had spread. It was on the elevator control panel, the laundry room dryer, the emergency exit sign on 12. Wherever it landed, it grew. A dime-sized sticker on a door knocker became a foot-wide plaque that bent the frame. The building shuddered like it was learning to breathe.  

We met in the janitor’s closet, our usual spot. Seven of us, shoulders touching, the smell of disinfectant and sweat. The superintendent, Mr. Halvorsen, had vanished three months prior, leaving behind a stack of unsigned work orders and a daughter, Emily, who still came every morning to water his plants. She’d started flirting with Mario, who hadn’t mentioned the tag’s growth to her. Not yet.  

“It’s not the tag,” said Priya, who managed the front desk. She held up a walkie-talkie crackling with tenant complaints. “It’s the schedule. We’ve been running on the summer timetable since October. The building knows.”  

We didn’t answer. The schedule was Emily’s fiction, a way to keep the tenants from rebelling while her father’s absence stretched on. We’d all agreed to it, but that didn’t mean we believed it.  

Mario spoke up. “The tag’s in the east stairwell now. It’s the size of a dinner plate.”  

We voted. Three wanted to rip it off every surface, burn the evidence. Four wanted to let it grow, see where it led. The tiebreaker was Luisa from 4B, who’d brought cookies once when we fixed her leak. She chose growth. “If it’s talking to the shower,” she said, “maybe it’s trying to tell us something.”  

By dawn, Tag 217B had colonized the roof. We watched from the stairwell as it pulsed, its numbers now a swirling script that hurt to look at. The building groaned again, louder. Somewhere below, a pipe burst.  

Mario found Emily in the lobby, her father’s plants wilting in her arms. “It’s not working,” he said. “The schedule. The tags. We can’t hold it.”  

She stared at the water pooling under the elevators. “He’s not coming back, is he?”  

He shook his head.  

The tag chose that moment to scream. A sound like sheet metal bending, and the entire building shuddered. The emergency lights flickered on.  

We regrouped. Priya suggested anchoring the tag with a counter-schedule—posting new dates, times, holidays, anything to confuse it. Luisa volunteered to climb the roof and scratch a new number onto it. Mario refused to let her go alone.  

They left at sunset. The rest of us waited, listening to the building’s joints creak.  

When they returned, their hands were blistered and their clothes smelled of ozone. The tag was gone. In its place, a single line of text on the roof door: *“Schedule void. New terms negotiated.”*  

Emily didn’t ask what they’d done. She just handed Mario a set of keys. “He left these in the desk,” she said. “You’re in charge now.”  

We didn’t cheer. We didn’t cry. We split up, each of us peeling off to their station. The building hummed, quieter now.  

That night, Mario and Luisa sat on the fire escape, sharing earbuds and a thermos of something warm. They didn’t talk about the tag or the schedule or the way the walls still felt like they might fold inward.  

Instead, Mario asked, “Do you think it’s really over?”  

Luisa bit her lip. “No.”  

He nodded. “Good. I wasn’t ready for it to be.”  

They sat until the streetlights buzzed off, two people who’d just broken every rule they’d lived by, starting the slow work of making new ones.  

Somewhere below, a pipe dripped. The building, for the first time in months, did not shudder.  

It waited.  

So did we.
