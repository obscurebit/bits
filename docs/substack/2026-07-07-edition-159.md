### The Wax Seal of Usual Practice

A notice hung above the filing cabinets, its letters faded but legible: *All documents pertaining to civic continuity must bear the Wax Seal of Usual Practice before processing. Deviation voids precedent.*  

Evelyn adjusted her apron, the brass seal press warming against her thigh. She’d oiled its hinge that morning, as she did every Tuesday, the routine as much a part of her as the scar on her knuckle from the time the press slipped. The workroom smelled of beeswax and stale coffee, the kind of place where the clocks never quite matched.  

“I need a permit for the bakery expansion,” said the man in the doorway, his overcoat dusted with flour. “The alderman’s office said I have to get it sealed here.”  

Evelyn didn’t recognize him. New faces usually meant trouble—either they didn’t know the rules or they knew them too well. She reached for the seal press. “Your original blueprints?”  

He handed them over, crisp at the edges. Too crisp. She glanced at the date stamp: last week. But the paper’s texture was off, like it had been aged in a hurry. A forgery, probably. But the seal would make it legitimate. That was the trick of the thing. Once pressed, the wax blemish on the document became law.  

Her brother-in-law, Leo, materialized beside her, reeking of bourbon and ink. “Let me,” he hissed, batting the papers away. “You’re too slow, Ev. Customers don’t like slow.” He clamped the press down, the wax pooling gold. “See? Easy.”  

The man left satisfied. Evelyn stared at the sealed document. The wax should’ve been scarlet for construction permits. Leo had used indigo, the color for zoning variances. A mistake? Or a favor for someone higher up?  

---  

Leo’s section of the workroom reeked of burnt hair. He kept a curling iron for “emergency alterations” to documents—burning edges, scorching ink to look older. He called it “contextual aging.”  

“Evelyn’s too soft,” he told the alderman’s courier, a boy no older than sixteen. “Thinks the rules are for *her*. But you and I—we know how things work.” He tossed the boy a sealed envelope, its wax a mottled green. Sanitation permit, though the paperwork inside was for a liquor license. The boy pocketed it without a word.  

Leo’s wife had left him six months ago, but Evelyn still fixed his coffee every morning. Habit. Or maybe she owed him for that time he’d covered her absence when her mother died. Favors outlasted love, in the Bureau of Civic Continuity.  

---  

The outsider returned at dusk. Same flour-dusted coat, but now he carried a stack of papers fanning out like a hand of cards. “More permits,” he said. “Expansion’s bigger than I thought.”  

Evelyn’s gut tightened. The documents were flawless forgeries: watermarks, ink bleed, even the faint smudge of a mayor’s signature from 1932. She reached for the press, but Leo snatched it.  

“Let’s not keep the man waiting,” he said, selecting a wax stick.  

“I’ll do it,” Evelyn snapped. The press was hers. The wax was hers. She chose carmine this time, proper for construction. The man’s eyes lingered on the color. A flicker of something—relief?—crossed his face.  

“Thank you,” he said, and for a moment, the warmth in his voice wasn’t about permits or forgeries. It was the kind of thanks that remembered her name.  

Leo sneered. “Sentiment won’t keep the lights on, Ev.”  

---  

The next morning, a crowd jammed the workroom. Ten people, all clutching papers sealed in mismatched waxes. Red, green, blue, yellow—all wrong. Evelyn’s scalp prickled. The outsider stood at the back, arms crossed.  

“Procedure’s procedure,” he said when Leo demanded explanations. “Your notice says any seal makes it official. I just… followed through.”  

Leo turned purple. “You can’t—you *can’t* just—”  

Evelyn saw it then: the man had used the chaos to seed doubt. If every color was valid, then no color was. The Bureau’s authority rested on the consistency of the seal. Without it, they were just clerks with hot metal.  

She stepped forward. “The press is broken,” she said. “Until further notice, no seals.”  

The crowd murmured. Leo gaped. The outsider’s eyes narrowed, but he didn’t protest.  

That night, Evelyn found a loaf of sourdough on her desk, still warm. No note. The bread was dense, with raisins—a kindness. She ate it slowly, the press cool and silent beside her.  

---  

Leo quit three days later. The outsider never returned. The notices stayed, but no one enforced them.  

Evelyn kept the seal press oiled, just in case.  

But when the alderman’s courier arrived, demanding a green-sealed permit for a factory that didn’t exist, she handed him a blank form and a pen.  

“Fill it yourself,” she said. “I’m out of wax.”  

He left. She didn’t care if he came back with a gun or a lawsuit.  

The press stayed closed.  

For once, the rules were quiet.
