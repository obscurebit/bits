## 📖 Today's Bit

### The Log That Knew Less

*Posted notice, yellowed and laminated, hangs above the checkpoint booth: “ALL PERSONAL DEVICES MUST BE LOGGED UPON ENTRY. FAILURE TO COMPLY WILL RESULT IN DELAY.” The words are in three languages, the third one smudged where someone tried to scratch out the Cyrillic.*  

We maintain the border’s oldest biometric scanner, a hulking rig with a cracked CRT monitor and a cable that hisses when it rains. It’s 1994, but here the future smells like scorched plastic and stale cigarettes. The scanner doesn’t read fingerprints anymore—it hasn’t since the firmware corrupted last winter—but no one’s dared report it. Not since Management started auditing departments that “underperform.”  

The device we’re not supposed to name is the log. A physical one: a dot matrix printout taped inside the booth, updated daily. It records every scan, every clearance, every “irregularity.” Except it doesn’t. The scanner’s been dead for months. We write the logs by hand now, in rotation, so no one person’s handwriting becomes suspicious. We’re careful to vary the times, the names, the “issues” we flag. A game of telephone with consequences.  

Lena wants to quit. She’s been saying it for weeks, ever since her brother’s papers came back rejected. “They’re making me choose,” she says, stirring instant coffee with a screwdriver. “Between the job and knowing what’s right.” She doesn’t say it loud. The booth’s walls are thin.  

The thing Lena doesn’t know—that none of them know—is that the log records more than we think. It doesn’t just take our handwritten notes. It takes the tremor in our hands when we write. The pause before we fake a name. The way Jarek always initialsthe denials with a loop in his “J” like he’s signing a love letter. It stores these in some forgotten subdirectory, a ghost protocol from the scanner’s original build.  

We find out the hard way.  

Management arrives on a Thursday, a woman in a wool coat that costs more than our monthly stipends. She carries a floppy disk. “Routine upgrade,” she says, but her eyes linger on the log. While she works, the monitor flickers to life. For the first time in months, it displays something real: a live feed of every lie we’ve written, annotated with our tells. Tremors, loops, erased words. A timeline of our compliance.  

She doesn’t say what she sees. Just hands Lena a citation for “discretionary inaccuracies” and leaves.  

That night, we gather in the booth. The log sits between us, its pages frayed. Jarek wants to burn it. Marika wants to mail it to the ombudsman. Lena just stares. We’ve all signed denials we didn’t believe. We’ve all written “cleared” next to names we knew would vanish into the system.  

The scanner hums, alive for the first time in months.  

Lena reaches for the maintenance tag attached to its side—a faded green card, its warranty long expired. She flips it over. On the back, in ink that’s bled through from the log’s pages, are her brother’s initials.  

We don’t speak. We don’t need to.  

One by one, we rip pages from the log. Feed them into the scanner’s mouth. It eats them without complaint.  

When the next transport arrives, we clear everyone. Every single one.  

Let Management come.  

We’ll be ready.  

With clean hands.
