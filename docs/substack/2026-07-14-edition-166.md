### The Tip That Outgrew Its Plate

"You’re covering her shift *again*?" Marco said, leaning against the snack cart’s dented aluminum counter. His name tag—*M. Delp*, faded ink, third reassignment—stuck to his chest like a stubborn burr.  

"I told you," I said, slotting the day’s first loyalty token into the cart’s grinder. It hummed, vibrating the tray where I’d set my lunch: a wilted turnover and a cup of synth-caffeine. "Lena’s kid’s got the flux. Again."  

"Her kid’s had the flux for six months." Marco nudged a raffle ticket tangled in the cart’s wheel. "You’re gonna have to stop pouring tokens into that thing someday. Management’s gonna notice."  

The grinder emitted a sound like a cat swallowing broken glass. I didn’t tell Marco that the machine *liked* Lena’s tokens. That they made it hum lower, like a contented beast. Or that I’d started feeding it mine too, just to keep the cart from shuddering when the building’s east wing expanded.  

---  

The building breathed. Everyone knew that. Tenants measured their apartments with tape measures and lies. Maintenance staff—me, Marco, Lena—adjusted the service corridors when the walls pinched too tight. But the tokens were different. They were *hers*.  

Lena had figured it out first. How a small mark on the edge of a token—a scratch from the grinder, a bite from the loyalty machine—could make a room grow. She’d shown me: a token scraped against the cart’s wheel, slipped into a wall slot, and suddenly the laundry room had an extra three feet of ceiling. "Useful," she’d said, grinning, "if you like ducking less."  

Management called it "settlement." We called it *hunger*. The building wanted tokens, marked ones, and if you didn’t feed it enough, it took what it wanted. A shelf here. A doorstep there.  

I’d started collecting discarded tickets, smoothing their edges, feeding them to the grinder to make new marks. Lena’s secret. Now hers and mine.  

---  

"You ever think," Marco said, watching me file a token’s edge with a fingernail, "that we’re just making it worse?"  

I didn’t answer. The cart’s grinder had started to *sing*, a high, eager note. The kind it made when the east wing was about to swell.  

"Management’s running a stress test next week." Marco lowered his voice. "They’ll see the discrepancies. The way the floors creak. They’ll shut us down."  

"Then we’ll lose our jobs," I said.  

"So?"  

The grinder spat out a token, now etched with a hairline crack. I held it up. Light bent around its edge, like it was a blade.  

"Or," I said, "they’ll want to know why the building’s still standing."  

---  

Lena’s kid didn’t have the flux. I knew that now.  

She’d been using her shifts to siphon tokens, marking them, feeding the building in secret places. The stairwell that never ended. The storage closet that became a greenhouse overnight. She’d been trying to *help* it grow, she said, when I confronted her.  

"Grow?" I’d laughed. "It’s eating the parking garage!"  

"So we’ll park elsewhere." She’d shrugged, but her eyes were bright, guilty. "It’s *alive*, isn’t it? Or near enough. And we’re just… maintenance."  

I should’ve reported her. Instead, I took her shifts. Fed the grinder. Watched the building bloom.  

---  

Marco found me at dusk, pressing a marked token into the service elevator’s control panel. The doors shuddered, then shrank, the metal curling like a fern leaf.  

"Stop," he said. "Just stop."  

I turned. The token in my hand glowed faintly, its crack deepening.  

"You think Lena wanted this?" I asked. "She’s got a *kid*. She just wanted a bigger room. A quiet place."  

"She’s making it unstable!"  

"So we make it stable." I held up the token. "We give it what it wants."  

Marco’s hand went to his name tag, as if it could anchor him. "You’re not even sure what it wants."  

The elevator doors finished shrinking. Behind them, the shaft yawned wider, deeper, as if the building had swallowed its own throat.  

I stepped back. The token in my palm pulsed, once.  

"Maybe it wants us to stop pretending it’s a building," I said.  

Marco didn’t leave. He never did. But he stopped arguing.  

---  

They found Lena’s tokens in the ventilation shafts. Management, finally curious. They called it "sabotage." Issued new, unmarkable cards.  

The building retaliated by shedding its skin. Hallway tiles curled like old posters. Windows fogged and cleared in patterns that looked like fingerprints.  

I kept one marked token. Press it into the snack cart’s grinder each morning. The cart hums. The east wing stays put.  

Sometimes, when the synth-caffeine’s bad, I think about Lena’s kid. How she never did have the flux. How Lena’s absence is a space that grows, quiet and relentless, in the shape of a mother who wanted more room to live.  

The token’s crack has spread. It spans the entire edge now, a line thin as a hair. I touch it, and the grinder sings.  

I tell myself it’s just maintenance.  

I tell myself a lot of things.  

The building listens. It always does.
