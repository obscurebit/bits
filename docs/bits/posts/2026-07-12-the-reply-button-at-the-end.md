# The Reply Button at the End of the World

The worst part of managing the hostel wasn’t the guests—it was the ghosts of their old profiles.  

I found Marco in the kitchen, staring at the mackerel-shaped token I’d sworn never to use again. It lay on the counter like a fish someone had forgotten to fry, its LED eyes blinking a balance I didn’t want to read.  

“You’re not still messing with that thing,” I said.  

Marco didn’t look up. “My sister’s rent just tripled. Her landlord says it’s because of ‘historical debt.’ From a game she played in 2012.”  

I poured two coffees, the kind that tastes like regret and Styrofoam. The token’s serial number glowed—MACKERELMEDIA #0421. I remembered the ads: *Catch virtual fish, earn real loyalty points!* Then the game vanished, leaving millions with balances that wouldn’t stay dead.  

“You know how this works,” Marco said. “I fix her debt, I lose my clearance. You fix it, they’ll blame the system on you.”  

Our manager, Ms. Vex, believed in two things: punctuality and punishing curiosity. Last month, she’d fired a janitor for asking why the vending machines sold mood stabilizers.  

I poked the token. It shuddered, spitting out a receipt: **DEBT ADJUSTMENT AVAILABLE. EMOTIONAL LABOR REQUIRED.**  

“Your sister ever play *Neverville*?” I asked.  

Marco winced. “She moderates their forum. Still. Thirteen years later.”  

The forum was a mausoleum of inside jokes and unpaid moderators. People posted just to hear the echo.  

I slid the token back. “I’ll do it. But you owe me a favor that doesn’t exist.”  

He nodded. Our friendship was a ledger neither of us could audit.  

The token’s eyes flashed red as I pressed it to the hostel’s payment terminal. The screen flickered, serving up a menu from a dead platform:  

**1. Forgive Debt (Temporary)**  
**2. Transfer Debt (Permanent)**  
**3. Complain to a Human (Not Recommended)**  

I chose 2. The receipt spat out a new balance: **$0.00. Condolences on your recent loss.**  

Marco stared. “What’d you do?”  

“Transferred her debt to someone who can afford it.”  

“Who?”  

I saluted the security camera in the corner. Ms. Vex would see the transfer, but the system would blame a “legacy error.” Let her explain *that* to corporate.  

Marco’s hands trembled. “You could get fired.”  

I laughed. “Worse comes to worst, I’ll moderate a dead game forum. Hear it’s great for the soul.”  

He almost smiled.  

Later, I found the token in my apron pocket, warm as a stone. Its eyes had gone dark. I dropped it in the vending machine, the one that still sold mood stabilizers.  

It ate the token and dispensed a single mackerel, fresh and silver, with a note: **Your debt is cleared. Sorry for your loss.**  

I ate the fish for dinner. Tasted like copper and closure.
