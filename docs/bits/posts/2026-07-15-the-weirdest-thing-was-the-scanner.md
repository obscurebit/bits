# The Weirdest Thing Was the Scanner

“You still using that brick from 2007?” Marlon says, leaning against the intake counter like he owns the dust. His name tag peels at the corner: *Corporate Liaison (West Region)*.  

You don’t look up. The scanner in your hand buzzes, its screen flickering the way it does when someone’s watching too hard. “It works,” you say. The lie tastes like the lint under your fingernails.  

Marlon snorts. “Works? Corporate’s sending a new system next week. Touchscreen. Cloud integration. Your little *vintage* act ends Monday.” He taps the counter, once, like a code.  

The scanner hums louder. You rotate it. The screen flashes: *22:17. 4 left.* Meaningless. You’ve learned to ignore the numbers when they mean nothing.  

Then the door chimes.  

She’s here.  

The woman from 3B. Always 3B. Always Thursday. Always a cardboard box held like it’s still full. You’ve sorted her donations for six months: cracked teacups, VHS tapes, a fur coat shedding like a dying cat. She never talks, just hands you the box and waits while you scan each item. Her eyes linger on the scanner’s screen. You think she knows.  

Today, the scanner doesn’t wait.  

As soon as her box hits the counter, the device shrieks—a sound like a fax machine being strangled. The screen floods with text: *THIS IS NOT THE LIFE YOU ORDERED. 88% CERTAINTY. RECOMMEND: ABORT.*  

Marlon’s head swivels. “What the hell is that?”  

You freeze. The woman from 3B doesn’t. She reaches out, presses her palm to the scanner’s screen. The text vanishes. It displays *THANK YOU. PLEASE WAIT* in clean, new font.  

Marlon squints. “Did you… train it to do that?”  

You don’t answer. The woman meets your eyes. For the first time, she speaks. “It only changes when someone cares,” she says. Her voice is a librarian’s: precise, tired. “He doesn’t care.” She nods at Marlon. “You do.”  

The scanner beeps. *APPROVED. TRANSFER COMPLETE.*  

Marlon frowns. “What’s approved?”  

Your face burns. The woman’s gone pale. You realize, suddenly, what she’s been donating. Not junk. *Choices.* Every item a decision point, a crossroads she didn’t take. The scanner’s her way of checking if she’s still on track. Your workaround—letting her “donate” these… what, echoes?—keeps her from unraveling.  

Marlon grabs the scanner. The screen goes blank. “This is company property. You’re not some kind of… archivist.” He sneers. “You’re an intake clerk.”  

The woman steps forward. “He’s the only one who didn’t laugh,” she says. “When I told him about the coat.”  

Marlon pauses. The coat had been his mother’s, donated after she died. You’d found a note in the pocket: *Keep it. She hated winters.* You’d slipped it into his inbox anonymously.  

The scanner buzzes again. *REASSIGN: 3B DONATIONS TO MARLON K. EFFECTIVE IMMEDIATELY.*  

“No,” the woman says.  

Marlon blinks. “This thing’s broken.”  

You reach for the scanner. It locks him out. Displays: *TRANSFER DENIED. EMOTIONAL STAKE INSUFFICIENT.*  

The woman laughs, sharp and broken. “Told you.”  

Marlon flushes. “I’m calling Support.”  

As he storms off, the scanner softens. *THANK YOU. NEXT DONATION: 7:00 PM.*  

The woman lingers. “They don’t get it, do they?”  

“No,” you say.  

She leaves.  

The scanner rests in your palm, warm. You think of the life you almost had: a sister who didn’t die, a mother who didn’t leave, a version where you fix things instead of just sorting them.  

Marlon returns with a clipboard. “Corporate says to log all anomalies.”  

You hand him the scanner. “It’s not an anomaly. It’s just… tired.”  

He scowls. “What’s it do?”  

You meet his eyes. “It knows who’s listening.”  

He pales. The scanner beeps once, a quiet *goodbye*.  

“Fine,” Marlon says. “Keep your relic.” He walks away.  

The door chimes. 3B’s next box waits.  

The scanner hums. *READY.*  

You wait.
