# The Good Book

"We didn’t need the book to tell us Mrs. Halvorsen was in trouble. But there it was, the coupon torn clean, sitting on the counter like a verdict."  

The back room of Moe’s Diner smelled like burnt coffee and shoe polish. Five of us crammed around a checkerboard table, the kind with peeling Formica that stuck to your thighs in summer. The coupon lay face-up, its edge frayed, the numbers stamped in red ink too sharp for the pale paper.  

"Same as last month," said Lorraine, her voice flat. She worked the register at Horn’s Grocery and knew how to make her words carry. "She uses it, the debt shows up tomorrow. On all our ledgers."  

Frank Sr., who’d hauled crates at the wholesale market since before the war, picked at the flaking varnish on the table. "Or we don’t honor it. Let her sweat." His son, Frankie, kicked the underside of the chair, a rhythm like a telegraph.  

We didn’t talk about the book like it was magic. It was just another thing, like the rotation for who covered the Kowalskis’ milk bill when their boy got sick. But five years back, when the coupons started showing what would happen instead of what had, everyone got real quiet for a week. Then we adjusted.  

I remembered the first time it changed. A woman from the tannery tried to use a coupon for her son’s hospital stay. The paper turned up blank. Turns out he died three days later. After that, we learned to read between the numbers.  

"Her daughter’s got the pneumonia," I said. "Landlord’s threatening eviction." The room hummed. We all knew the math. Honor the coupon, and the debt would bloom like mold on wallpaper—everyone’s shares shrinking, the collective ledger stained. Let it lapse, and Mrs. Halvorsen would be out on the sidewalk with her asthma and her pride, and her daughter’s cough would turn wet and worse.  

Frankie stopped kicking. "Ain’t that what the triage fund’s for?"  

"Fund’s dry," Lorraine said.  

Outside, a streetcar clattered past. The light from the bulb above us flickered, and for a second, the coupon seemed to shimmer. I thought of my sister, home with her sprained ankle and her stories about the new job at the telephone exchange. She didn’t know about the book. We’d agreed: one more day of her believing she’d earned it.  

"Vote," Frank Sr. said.  

Three hands went up to honor the coupon. Two held back.  

Lorraine slid the coupon toward me. "You’re the closest to neutral."  

I touched the edge. The paper was warm.  

In the movies, they make it sound like a lightning bolt. But the choice just settled, like sediment in water.  

"We do it," I said. "But we spread the debt. Five-way split."  

The room exhaled. Frankie started tapping again.  

When we walked Mrs. Halvorsen to the pharmacy, the coupon in hand, the pharmacist didn’t ask questions. He never did. The system was the system.  

Later, I checked my ledger. The debt was there, a red number like a smudge of jam. I added the new line item: *Halvorsen, E. – $14.50 (shared)*.  

That night, my sister called. "They love me here," she said. "I’m learning the switches so fast."  

I didn’t tell her the job didn’t exist. Not yet.  

The book doesn’t show everything. Just what’s stitched to the threads we pull.  

We’ll fix it tomorrow. Maybe.
