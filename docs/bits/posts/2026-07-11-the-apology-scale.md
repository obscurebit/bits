# The Apology Scale

A notice taped to the cracked linoleum counter read: *All rations require verification by the Apology Scale. Refusals will be reported to the Ministry of Calorific Equity.* The words were handwritten in smudged pencil, the kind of correction that meant someone higher up had changed their mind again.  

Maribelle thumbed the scale’s rusted lever. Nothing. The dial stayed frozen at 0.5 kilograms, though the basket held only a withered turnip and a fist of rice. The woman on the other side of the counter—Toothless Marta, who’d traded her dentures for a month’s worth of painkillers last winter—drummed her fingers. “Ain’t got all day,” she muttered.  

Maribelle glanced at the doorway. No sign of him yet. Good.  

The scale shuddered. A voice, creaky as the building’s pipes, rasped from its speaker: **“Apology required. Relationship: Maribelle and Dexter. Status: Unresolved.”**  

Marta blinked. “That thing’s been broken for months. Just write the number yourself like usual.”  

Maribelle hesitated. The Ministry’s last auditor had caught her “adjusting” figures. She’d only fudged by pennies—rounded a 0.3 to a 0.5, a 0.7 to a 1.0—to keep the peace. But Dexter hadn’t believed her. Or maybe he had. Either way, he’d written her up.  

Now he was coming back.  

The scale whirred, insistent. **“Apology required.”**  

Marta leaned in. “You two used to be close, yeah? Back when the tap water still ran hot.”  

Maribelle jabbed the lever again. The scale shuddered, then spat out a slip of paper: *Apology must include: (1) Admission of theft (2) Return of item (3) Acknowledgment of emotional damages.*  

Theft? She’d taken a single cinnamon stick from the pantry three years ago. Dexter had eaten the last of the sugar.  

Footsteps echoed in the hall.  

“Here,” Maribelle hissed, dumping Marta’s rations into a frayed sack. “Take it. I’ll deal with the scale.”  

Marta didn’t argue.  

Dexter stood in the doorway, clipboard pressed to his chest like a shield. His vest was newer than hers, the buttons not yet frayed. “Heard the scale’s acting up,” he said.  

Maribelle forced a smile. “Just needs a kick.”  

He stepped closer. The scale’s voice boomed: **“Apology required. Relationship: Dexter and Maribelle. Status: Unresolved.”**  

Dexter’s eyes narrowed. “You never said sorry.”  

“For what?”  

“The cinnamon. The reports. You told the Ministry I was sleeping on duty.”  

“You were.”  

“And you weren’t? You think I don’t know about your little ‘rounding’?”  

The scale screeched: **“Direct eye contact required. Sincerity metric: 0%.”**  

Marta’s voice floated back from the hall: “Just lie to it. Everyone does.”  

Maribelle met Dexter’s gaze. His left eyelid twitched, the way it had when he’d lied about finishing his certification. “I’m sorry I didn’t tell you about the cinnamon,” she said.  

The scale hummed. **“Insufficient. Item not returned.”**  

Dexter’s mouth thinned. “It was my mother’s cinnamon.”  

“So?”  

“She died two weeks later. You think I’d have taken that sugar if I knew?”  

The room seemed to dampen, as if the walls had exhaled. Maribelle’s throat tightened. She’d never asked about his mother.  

A chipped coffee mug sat on the counter—Dexter’s old one, left behind when he’d been promoted. Stained with the residue of a thousand brews, its handle glued with threadbare duct tape. He’d drunk from it the night they’d split, arguing over whose turn it was to scrub the mildew from the staff shower.  

“You want the cinnamon back?” she said.  

“It’s in the pantry. I put it there this morning.”  

The scale groaned. **“Apology must be spoken into the receptor.”**  

Maribelle picked up the mug. Held it out. “I’m sorry,” she said, too quiet.  

**“Sincerity metric: 12%.”**  

Dexter didn’t take the mug. “You don’t get to be sorry and keep the favor.”  

“Favor?”  

“You think I didn’t notice you rerouted the heater to your office last winter? While the infirmary froze?”  

The mug’s glue gave way. The handle came off in her hand.  

Maribelle stared at the scale. At the numbers that would balance if she just—  

She grabbed Dexter’s clipboard, flipped to the inspection form. “Here,” she said, thrusting it forward. “Write it yourself. I’ll sign.”  

He didn’t move.  

Somewhere, a pipe burst. Water hissed through the ceiling tiles.  

**“Relationship status: Unresolved,”** the scale repeated.  

Maribelle reached past it, yanked the power cord. The voice died. The dial fluttered down to 0.  

Dexter sighed. “They’ll replace it tomorrow.”  

“I’ll handle it.”  

He left.  

Maribelle filled out Marta’s ration slip by hand. Wrote 0.5.  

The dampness in the walls thickened. She kept writing, the pencil point snapping twice, the numbers blurring.  

The mug’s broken handle pricked her palm. She didn’t notice.
