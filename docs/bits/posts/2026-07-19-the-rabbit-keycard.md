# The Rabbit Keycard

The first rule of the Vending Machine Technician’s Handbook is that there are no rules. The second is that you never use the master keycard for personal purchases. Both are lies.  

I learned this my third day on the job, when the power flickered and the old soda machine on 7th Street started dispensing Freckle Colas for free. The keycard—a chipped plastic thing with a faded rabbit logo—had jammed in the slot. I yanked it out, cursed, and shoved it back. The machine shuddered, then spat out a drink. No payment, no scan. Just the rabbit’s glitchy wink.  

Darrell saw me test it later. He was restocking the chip aisle, his name tag crooked on his vest. “You just gave that kid a free candy bar,” he said, voice flat. “With the rabbit card.”  

“It was a *test*,” I said. “Machine’s broken.”  

“Uh-huh.” He tossed a bag of salt-and-vinegar chips onto the shelf. “You test it again, I’m reporting you.”  

But he didn’t. Two days later, I caught him using the keycard to get a granola bar for a woman who’d forgotten her wallet. Her kid was crying, fists smeared with bubblegum-scented hand sanitizer. Darrell slid the bar across the counter like it was nothing.  

“You’re such a hypocrite,” I said afterward.  

He spat into the trash can. “I’m a pragmatist. You’re the one who thinks stealing’s a favor.”  

The rabbit keycard became our quiet currency. A candy bar for the nurse who stayed late at the clinic. A soda for the guy who fixed the streetlights. We never talked about it. We didn’t have to. The card’s glitch was a hole in the world, and we slipped things through it like letters into a mailbox.  

Then Vlad showed up.  

He wore a suit that screamed *I own things*, his shoes clean enough to offend in a town where dust clung to everything. “You owe me,” he said, leaning on the counter, “for the time I covered your brother’s debt.”  

My brother had died six months ago, his name still on the clinic’s unpaid ledger. I nodded.  

“I need a favor,” Vlad said. “A dozen energy drinks. For my boys.”  

The drinks weren’t for his boys. They were for the men who collected “taxes” from the food bank. I knew because I’d seen them count the cans behind the old library.  

I used the rabbit card. Twelve drinks, no charge. Vlad smiled like he’d won.  

Darrell found me after, shaking his head. “You just built him a bridge. He’ll walk across it every damn day now.”  

“He’ll hurt someone else if I don’t,” I said.  

“He’ll hurt someone *because* you did.”  

The next week, three more guys came in, each flashing a rabbit keycard they’d somehow copied. The machines started glitching citywide. A barista at the café across town got fired for “theft” when her keycard duplicated. The hole in the world had grown, and now it was swallowing people.  

I went to Vlad. “Stop. You’re breaking it.”  

He laughed. “Breaking it? Kid, you *built* this. You think kindness doesn’t have a price?”  

Darrell quit the next day. Left his vest on the snack cart. I found him later at the bus station, his suitcase lined with granola bars and soda cans.  

“Where you going?” I asked.  

“Somewhere the rabbits don’t multiply,” he said.  

I stayed. I used the keycard one last time—bought every expired candy bar on the shelf, dumped them into a trash bag, and set it on fire in the alley. The smoke rose like an argument.  

The handbook’s third rule, unwritten, is that every cheat becomes a column in the ledger. You just get to decide what it’s worth.  

I’m writing this down because the fire department’s here now, asking about the blaze. They’ll blame the rats, or the power grid, or whatever’s convenient. But if you listen close, you can still hear the rabbit’s teeth clicking in the machines.  

It’s never been about the keycard.  

It’s about who gets to decide what’s broken.
